Participatory Journalism and Its Potential in AI-Assisted Local News

Knight First Amendment Institute | January 9, 2026

AI in local journalism is more likely to yield civic benefits if it begins with the culture and needs of the community. Drawing on City Bureau's "Documenters" program, this essay argues that adopting AI tools should go hand in hand with implementing more equitable, civically-focused, participatory forms of journalism—not just making traditional journalism more efficient.

Research

I study how local news affects political behavior, how campaigns strategize in different media environments, and how we can strengthen local journalism as civic infrastructure. My work combines rigorous experimental methods with real-world impact.

Local News & Democracy

What happens to communities when local news disappears? My research examines how local media shapes political knowledge, participation, and polarization. I've studied newspaper closures, news deserts, and the civic consequences of declining local journalism.

Key Finding: When local newspapers close, communities see decreased political engagement and increased partisan polarization, with voters relying more on national partisan news.

Campaign Strategy

How do political campaigns adapt to changing media landscapes? I analyze field office placement, advertising strategies, and how campaigns target voters in different information environments.

Key Finding: Presidential campaigns strategically locate field offices in areas with local newspapers to maximize media coverage and volunteer mobilization.

News Innovation & Engagement

What newsroom innovations actually work? Through experimental research with local news organizations, I test audience engagement strategies, content approaches, and sustainable journalism models.

Key Finding: Testing is revealing what draws audiences to local news and how newsrooms can build sustainable relationships with their communities.

Media Effects & Polarization

How does media consumption shape political attitudes and behavior? I study partisan media effects, TV news coverage patterns, and how different media choices influence voters' understanding of politics.

Key Finding: TV news systematically overrepresents ideologically extreme members of Congress, making American politics appear more polarized than it actually is.
